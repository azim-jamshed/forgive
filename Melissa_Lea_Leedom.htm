BIOGRAPHY

Melissa Lea Leedom is a native of New Orleans, and has also lived in Florida, Virginia, Alabama, and Maryland. She holds degrees in English and Professional Writing from Troy State University at Dothan, Alabama, and Towson University in Maryland.

Ms. Leedom was single-again for over five years and was a member of Single Adult Sunday School classes in New Orleans and the Metro-D.C. area. She lives in a suburb of Austin, TX.

INTERVIEW with Melissa Lea Leedom

1. What do you hope to accomplish through your writing?

Jesus said that we are to be "salt and light" to the world around us. I want the characters in my stories to embody those Christian characteristics. I want to show real, everyday people striving to live as Christ would have them live, and have them interact in stories where their relationship with Christ affects their relationships with each other. When I decided to write my first novel, I knew I wanted to write a love story, but I decided there were enough writers out there writing the amoral stuff. I thought it was time somebody wrote stories about Christian singles and showed that it is possible to operate within the Christian set of values in a modern setting. I hadn't seen very much of that in novels when I started writing.

2. Are any of your characters based on real people?

Yes, a few. I actually did know a woman in her early thirties who had been widowed with two young children, and I often wondered what it would be like to be in her position, how long it might take before I would be ready to see someone new. That provided the germ of a story idea for To Forgive, Divine. Also, when we lived in Alabama, I was on a local board of directors of a Habitat for Humanity chapter. That was the idea behind the Chandler City Benevolent Council. I have been blessed to have known several wonderful pastors. Jeff Wells is a composite and a tribute to these wonderful men. There is a little bit of my grandmother in Ruth Newton, a little bit of my own best friend in Cheryl, and a little bit of me in several of the characters!

3. How do you create a story?

The characters come first. I think of people I'd like personally, who'd be in a situation to which I'd be sympathetic, like Bonnie Callaway's. Then I start playing "What if?" "What if such-and-such a person entered her life?" "What roadblocks could be thrown in the way?" "What situations would bring this couple back into each other's company?" The complicating situations are what created the subplots and what helped to enrich the story in To Forgive, Divine. But it also served the purpose of showing Bonnie and Jeff at work in their Christian lives. In Book II (no title yet), we'll see Carolyn Perkins and Jenny Pierce in their own stories.

4. At what age did you decide you wanted to be a writer?

I've always liked to write, since about the fourth or fifth grade. But I don't think I was really aware that a writer was something you could "be" like a teacher or a nurse or a pilot, until I was well into my 20s. I guess I never really gave much thought about where books came from. It wasn't until I was browsing in a bookstore when I lived in Washington and stumbled onto Writer's Digest that I got the idea that you could do something with the stories you scribbled down (I used to like to write long-hand!), and that perhaps you could even make a living at it! (I've heard that some do...)

5. What advice would you offer to a beginning author?

Read as much as you can of the genre you'd like write. Try to internalize the patterns in the plotting--see how things fit and flow together, see how the author uses foreshadowing.

Assess your skill level. If you weren't good at spelling and grammar in school, enroll in a community college course and brush up your skills. Invest in a couple of good reference books to have handy as you write.

Write. Write as much as you can. Don't edit as you go. Get your story out of you on paper. Let your characters live their lives, be who they are. Write your story or your book, then set it aside for a month. Then go back and begin re-writing. If you have the benefit of a critique group of one or two other knowledgeable people, take advantage of it. But follow your instincts about your characters.

6. Who do you think are your greatest influences?

Without a doubt, the Sunday School teachers, youth ministers, and pastors I had as a teenager were my greatest influences. I am grateful to have been the recipient of so much love and dedication. As the song says, "I am a life that was changed; I am so glad you gave."

7. What do you think it means to be a Christian writer?

I can only say what it means to me, or what I think it should mean. C.S. Lewis said, "Everything I write is evangelistic." That should be the goal of every Christian writer, that somehow, through his or her work, the saving message of Christ would shine through and that readers would know that Jesus Christ is Lord.

8. What is the single greatest mistake you think authors make today?

Christian authors or secular authors, or both? If I had to narrow it down to Christian authors, I have to say I see a tendency in many books to "secularize." Stories are "clean," in that there is no overt immorality, but there is little or no mention of Christ, so that "inspirational" stories are more palatable to a mass audience. I think that is a mistake. We cannot be salt if we have lost our savor.

10. What do you think is the most rewarding part of your work?

I always enjoy speaking to groups and seeing the response of Christian singles to a book about "them." I think (and apparently so do many of them) that they are the most underrepresented group in Christian fiction.
